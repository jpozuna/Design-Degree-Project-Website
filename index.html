Increasing the visibility at the John D. O'Bryant African American Institute

01 — Problem Space

The Visibility Challenge

Students at Northeastern know it only as a building but it's so much more than that. The John D. O'Bryant African American Institute offers vital support services, cultural programming, and community spaces. Yet its identity remains invisible to the very students who need it most.

The Core Problem

Students transitioning to a predominantly white institution lack awareness of dedicated cultural and academic support spaces. Without knowing the Institute exists, they cannot access its resources or community.

Why Word of Mouth Fails

Traditional organic marketing doesn't work when students don't know what exists. The most isolated students are precisely those missed by social networks.

Target Audience

First-year students, transfer students, and students from underrepresented backgrounds navigating the transition to Northeastern, seeking community and belonging.

How can we help students discover and engage with the Institute as a community space for celebration, cultural gathering, and academic support—not just another building on campus?

02 — Impact & Solutions

A Comprehensive Welcome Packet System

One solution is to provide an accessible entry point for students unaware of the Institute. A welcome packet containing three physical touchpoints which are the brochure, engagment stamp book and stickers guide students from discovery to engagement.

Brochure

The first touchpoint: a brochure that introduces students to the Institute's history, mission, and available resources. Designed for discovery and initial awareness.

  • Concise overview of services and programs
  • Clear service categories and contact information
  • Historical context rooted in 1968 founding
  • Visual identity establishing brand recognition

Engagement Stamp Book

Inspired by Japanese goshuin temple books, this artifact transforms Institute visits into a collectible journey, encouraging ongoing participation and community building.

  • Space for event stamps and personal reflection
  • Gamification of community participation
  • Physical memory-keeper for student experiences

Stickers

Identity markers that create visibility across campus and foster pride in Institute affiliation.

  • Pan-African color palette for cultural celebration
  • Walking advertisements across campus

Additional Deliverables

Beyond the welcome packet, the project includes physical signage and an interactive digital display.

Stained Glass Entrance Signage

A conspicuous physical marker at the Institute's entrance that transforms the space from just another building to identifiable cultural destination.

  • Stained glass design incorporating vibrant colors and celebrating African culture
  • Establishes visual presence
  • Creates sense of arrival and welcome

Interactive Display

A digital touchpoint to highlight the Institute's history, provide an accessible method to quickly give students relevant information, and encourage exploration of resources.

  • Provides historical context from founding to present
  • Gameifies learning about the Institute's history
  • Students have access to engage with Institute's resources
03 — Design Process

From Research to Reality

A human-centered approach grounded in cultural research, user journey mapping, and iterative refinement informed by feedback and historical context.

Phase 1

Research & Discovery

Deep dive into the Institute's history. Explored student needs through informal interviews and observation, identifying the gap between available resources and student awareness.

Key insight: Students don't seek what they don't know exists.

Phase 2

User Journey Mapping

Mapped the student experience from initial interaction through engagement. Identified three main moments: initial discovery, first visit, and sustained engagement.

This journey map revealed the need for multiple touchpoints addressing different user needs at different stages.

Phase 3

Concept Development & Sketching

Explored multiple concepts through rapid sketching.

04 — Research Insights

What I Learned

Discovery Gap

Students cannot seek support from resources they don't know exist. The Institute's location right below West Village F masks its unique identity and purpose, reducing it to just another campus building in students' mental maps.

Word of Mouth Limitations

Organic marketing fails when there's no initial awareness to spread. The students most isolated from social networks,often those who would benefit most from the Institute, are precisely those missed by word-of-mouth strategies.

Physical Presence Matters

Tangible, branded materials provide visual reinforcement and added cognitive weight to students' minds.

Third Space Necessity

Students need spaces beyond academic and residential environments for celebration, cultural gathering, and informal community building. The Institute can serve this function but only if students know it exists and feel welcomed.

Students transitioning to predominantly white institutions carry the weight of migration—seeking community spaces where they can celebrate culture, find support, and build belonging.

06 — Cultural Foundation

Drawing from Jacob Lawrence's Legacy

A lot of visual inspiration was drawn from Jacob Lawrence's artistic exploration of migration, community, and cultural spaces.

The Migration Series

Lawrence's 60-panel series documenting the Great Migration of African Americans from the rural South to urban North parallels contemporary student migration to predominantly white institutions. Both involve leaving familiar communities, seeking opportunity, and navigating new cultural landscapes while maintaining identity and connection to heritage.

The geometric compositions and flattened forms in Lawrence's panels informed the visual language of this project.

Pool Parlor (1942)

This specific work captures a community gathering space. The pool hall serves as a "third space" beyond home and work, a place for leisure, conversation, and cultural exchange.

The Institute can function similarly: not just an academic support center, but a place where students gather, celebrate, and build community.
